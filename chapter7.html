Chapter 7: Classification II - OBIA, Subpixel Analysis, and Accuracy Assessment

Abstract

Object-Based Image Analysis (OBIA) represents an innovative method for classifying remote sensing imagery, significantly surpassing traditional pixel-based techniques by analyzing images through segmented, meaningful geographic objects. This method integrates spectral data alongside spatial context, shape, texture, and hierarchical information, enhancing accuracy, especially in complex and heterogeneous landscapes. Complementing OBIA, subpixel analysis addresses the mixed-pixel problem common in medium-to-coarse resolution imagery. Techniques such as spectral unmixing quantify fractional abundances of multiple land-cover classes within individual pixels, enabling the detection of components smaller than sensor resolution. Integrating OBIA and subpixel analysis thus yields more detailed and accurate land-cover maps crucial for ecological monitoring and urban planning.

Accuracy assessments of these advanced classification methods typically involve constructing error matrices (confusion matrices), summarizing agreement between ground truth and classified results. Key derived accuracy metrics include the Kappa coefficient, Producerâ€™s Accuracy (PA), Userâ€™s Accuracy (UA), and Overall Accuracy (OA). The Kappa coefficient adjusts classification accuracy for chance agreement, whereas Producerâ€™s Accuracy indicates the probability of correctly classifying reference data, reflecting omission errors. Userâ€™s Accuracy represents reliability from a userâ€™s perspective, highlighting commission errors. Overall Accuracy, an intuitive metric, measures correctly classified pixels across all classes, providing an overall evaluation.

Application

OBIA combined with subpixel analysis has widespread applications in environmental and urban management. In urban areas, OBIA effectively extracts impervious surfaces, vegetation, and water bodiesâ€”land covers where spectral mixing is common. Subpixel methods, such as spectral mixture analysis (SMA), further enhance OBIA results by quantifying fractional land-cover within mixed pixels. For instance, Chen et al. (2018) successfully applied OBIA combined with LiDAR and very-high-resolution imagery for urban classification, clearly outperforming pixel-based methods in estimating impervious surfaces and vegetation coverage.

Fig 2.1 Chen et al. (2018) successfully applied OBIA with SMA to improve urban impervious surface estimation

Accuracy assessment using error matrices and Kappa coefficients is indispensable in OBIA research. Myint et al. (2011) provided comprehensive evaluations of OBIA-based urban land-use classification employing confusion matrices and Kappa statistics, offering robust quantitative evidence of classification reliability. Spatial cross-validation methods have further proven crucial in addressing spatial autocorrelation. Pohjankukka et al. (2017) demonstrated how spatial k-fold cross-validation significantly improved accuracy assessments over traditional random splits by explicitly accounting for spatial dependency, thus providing more reliable validation for classification models.

Reflection

Despite considerable advances in classification accuracy through OBIA and subpixel analysis, methodological and practical challenges persist. OBIA accuracy critically depends on segmentation parameters such as scale, shape, and compactness, demanding expert knowledge for effective calibration. Incorrect parameter choices can severely impact classification accuracy, limiting OBIAâ€™s practical application by non-specialists. Similarly, subpixel analysis accuracy hinges on correctly identifying spectral endmembers, which can vary spatially and temporally. Misidentifying endmembers reduces accuracy, potentially biasing classification outcomes.

Although widely used, accuracy metrics like the Kappa coefficient have limitations, notably sensitivity to prevalence effects and class imbalance. Recent research recommends employing alternative metrics such as Producerâ€™s/Userâ€™s Accuracy or Overall Accuracy for more stable assessments. Furthermore, spatial cross-validation, despite its effectiveness, requires substantial computational resources and carefully constructed spatial partitions. Poorly executed spatial partitions can inadvertently introduce biases, compromising validation reliability.

Future technological innovations could alleviate these challenges. Deep learning-based segmentation methods could automate parameter selection in OBIA, simplifying workflows considerably. Advances in hyperspectral and super-resolution remote sensing imagery might enhance spectral endmember identification, significantly improving subpixel analysis accuracy. Further, employing robust accuracy metrics and AI-driven automated spatial partitioning algorithms could enhance validation processesâ€™ efficiency and reliability. These innovations would broaden OBIA and subpixel analysesâ€™ adoption, significantly contributing to environmental management and informed policy-making.
